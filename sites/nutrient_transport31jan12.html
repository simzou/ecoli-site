Nutrient Uptake

Nutrient uptake (i.e., solute transport) is a cellular process for acquiring molecules from the cell environment that are needed to support cell growth and metabolism.

Key Concepts 

  • Cells require many types of essential and non-essential nutrients.
  • Cells can scavenge low abundant compounds from their environments and accumulate them intracellularly.
  • Two types/classes of nutrient uptake (solute transport) are distinguished:
  • Passive transport: does not require energy.  Passive transport occurs either by passive diffusion across membranes or via facilitated diffusion which is mediated by membrane intrinsic proteins.
  • Active transport: requires dedicated transporters and energy input. Transporters involved in active transport are differentiated based on their mechanism as ABC-type transporters, symporters and antiporters.

Specific information on E. coli Nutrient Uptake

I. Passive transport across the cytoplasmic membrane

Entry of solutes into the cell via diffusion is generally limited as the cytoplasmic membrane presents a selective hydrophobic barrier. The driving force of simple diffusion is the solute concentration gradient across the membrane. Here, we describe two types of diffusion processes across the membrane that differ by whether a membrane protein assists in the movement. 

1. Passive diffusion. 

2. Facilitated diffusion

  • Porins
  • Carrier proteins       

II. Active transport across the cytoplasmic membrane

The majority of molecules required for cell metabolism are taken up by active transport systems. This process is carried out transporter proteins located in the cytoplasmic membrane and always requires the expenditure of metabolic energy in the form of ATP or the the proton motive force. Some sugar transporters utilize the high energy compound PEP (phosphoenylpyruvate). Active transport systems are highly specific for an individual molecule or class of structurally related compounds. Since active transport is driven by cell energy, it allows the cell to accumulate molecules to high concentrations inside the cytoplasm relative to the level outside the cell (i.e., molecules are accumulated against an increasing concentration gradient). Finally, the synthesis and/or activity of many of these active transport systems are regulated depending on the cellular needs. 

Types of Active Transport Systems

Active transport systems are divided into three classes and are generally named according to their mechanism of action.  In most cases the solute is merely transported from the cell exterior to the interior cytoplasm in an unmodified form. In a few cases the solute is chemically altered during its transport.

1. ABC transporters.

2. Secondary transport systems or permeases.

  • Symporters
  • Antiporters

3. Group translocation systems

  • The phosphotransferase system (PTS)
  • E. coli fatty acid transport
